About Me & This Space

With a foundation in textile design, my career began in the world of creative practice, exploring hands-on making through workshops and exhibitions. This passion for creativity naturally evolved into a love for teaching, leading me to TEFL and community education, where I have had the privilege of working with diverse learners across various educational landscapes. Throughout this journey, my core focus has remained unchanged: a deep interest in creative pedagogy, experiential learning, and fostering meaningful engagement.

Welcome to my reflective journal. As I immerse myself in this course, I am focusing on deepening my understanding of pedagogy, particularly through the lens of reflective practice and digital learning tools. Over the next thirteen weeks, this site will serve as a dynamic space to post my reflections, think aloud about what is working, navigate challenges, and track how my thinking shifts. I am incredibly excited to weave my experiences in creative design and teaching together with these new horizons in digital education.

May 11 – May 17, 2026
Week 1 May 17, 2026 reflection

A Reflection on Why We Click

Every time a new digital tool is introduced, I experience a brief moment of hesitation. My mind immediately turns to Davis’s (2017) Technology Acceptance Model (TAM). Instead of focusing on technical details, I quietly ask myself: Will this actually make my life easier, or will I spend hours struggling to figure it out? I’ve realized that Perceived Usefulness and Perceived Ease of Use are deeply emotional experiences. If a tool feels frustrating, it becomes difficult to see its value, no matter how advanced it is.

Once I become comfortable with a tool, the real learning begins.This is where the TPACK framework becomes meaningful. It reminds me that technological knowledge is hollow if it is disconnected from pedagogy and content. As Power & Kay (2023) highlights post-pandemic faculty adaptation, while professional development builds our pedagogical design skills, it is our peer communities of practice that best support how we blend technology with actual content. Without this support, we often fall into simple substitution on the SAMR ladder, using digital tools simply because they are available, without transforming the learning experience.

This human element of technology adoption is further illuminated by Rogers’ (1962) Diffusion of Innovations theory. While I am eager to experiment with new tools, I understand why others hesitate, especially when change feels imposed. Understanding the adoption curve helped me see that resistance is not inherently negative; people simply need time, stability, or a clearer demonstration of an innovation's true value. By anchoring these adoption theories in frameworks like Universal Design for Learning and the ISTE Standards, we can shift the focus from mere tech survival to intentional, inclusive design. The Journal of Educational Informatics article beautifully synthesized this fluid reality.The post-pandemic shift permanently disrupted linear lesson planning (Musa et al., 2024).

Today's learning environments are unpredictable and responsive, a reality accelerated by Generative AI. These emerging AI tools are no longer passive resources; they respond, adapt, and influence learning in real time, making the experience feel genuinely collaborative. This active partnership challenges traditional TPACK boundaries because the technology component is no longer a static variable, but a dynamic, constantly evolving co-creator.

Ultimately, this week reinforced that adopting digital tools is fundamentally shaped by human emotions, institutional diffusion, and perceived value. Whether navigating the adoption curve or partnering with generative AI, our goal must extend beyond basic acceptance toward meaningful classroom implementation, centering human connection, accessibility, and thoughtful pedagogical decision-making in increasingly digital spaces.

Week 3 May 31, 2026 reflection

A Strategic Reflection on Innovation and Pedagogical Resilience

In my previous reflection, I explored why we click with new technologies, arguing that our willingness to adopt digital tools is deeply influenced by human emotion, personal experiences, and comfort zones. This week, however, my perspective shifted from the individual to the collective. Rather than asking why educators embrace or resist innovation, I found myself considering a broader question: How does individual acceptance evolve into meaningful systemic change?

Clayton Christensen's Theory of Disruptive Innovation provides a compelling lens through which to examine this question. Christensen argues that established organizations often focus on meeting the needs of their mainstream users, causing them to overlook emerging innovations that initially appear inferior or insufficient. While this theory emerged from the business sector, I was struck by how closely it mirrors the realities of education. Schools frequently prioritize technologies that are proven, familiar, and aligned with existing structures, making it difficult for emerging innovations to gain traction despite their transformative potential. Meaningful progress, therefore, requires a willingness to experiment, embrace uncertainty, and engage with ideas before their value is fully realized.

The COVID-19 pandemic brought this tension into sharp focus. As a sudden disruption to long-established educational practices, it exposed both the strengths and vulnerabilities of our systems. While educators demonstrated remarkable adaptability during the transition to online learning, the readings challenged my assumption that resistance to technology is primarily a matter of attitude or unwillingness to change. Instead, they suggest that a more significant barrier is what has been described as the "self-confidence problem", a lack of teacher self-efficacy. Educators may recognize the value of technology yet still feel uncertain about their ability to integrate it effectively into their teaching.

This distinction is important because successful technology integration requires more than technical competence. Teachers must develop the technical-pedagogical knowledge necessary to make informed instructional decisions about when, why, and how digital tools can enhance learning. The challenge, therefore, is not simply learning to use technology but developing the confidence and pedagogical expertise to use it purposefully. This realization shifted my thinking about educational innovation. Rather than viewing change as a top-down process driven by policy or technology itself, I now see it as a collaborative and deep social process that depends on ongoing dialogue, shared learning, and collective problem-solving among educators and students.

Ultimately, these sources helped me challenge the common misconception that innovation is a singular "lightning bolt" of genius reserved for a select group of technological visionaries. Instead, innovation in education is best understood as an iterative and often unremarkable process of experimentation, reflection, adaptation, and continuous improvement (Bates, 2012). This insight has important implications for my future practice. If innovation is a process rather than a breakthrough event, then educators should not be expected to achieve perfection immediately. Instead, schools and institutions must create conditions that support responsible risk-taking, collaborative inquiry, and learning from failure.

If we hope to move beyond isolated pockets of innovation, where a small number of enthusiastic educators carry the burden of change, institutions must play a more active role. Innovation should be embedded within academic planning, professional learning, and organizational culture. Equally important, teachers must be provided with both the financial resources and psychological safety necessary to experiment without fear of failure. Only by fostering a culture of shared inquiry and collective efficacy can we transform the lessons of the digital pivot into lasting educational resilience.

Reflections on Educational Technology, AI, and Accessibility

This week's presentations offered a powerful exploration of some of the most pressing issues in educational technology and innovation. Each presentation challenged me to think critically about how technology is shaping learning, teaching, and inclusion in today's world.

Over-Reliance on Short-Form Learning

Our group's presentation focused on the growing challenge of over-reliance on short-form learning. We examined how platforms such as TikTok and YouTube Shorts encourage constant context-switching and passive information consumption, making it more difficult to develop the deep focus and memory integration required for meaningful learning.

Researching this topic was both enlightening and unsettling. I began to recognize many of these patterns in my own digital habits. The urge to check my phone during moments of concentration made me reflect on how easily attention can be fragmented. More importantly, it raised concerns about the environment my children are growing up in, where algorithms are designed to maximize engagement through constant stimulation.

As a family, we are making small but intentional changes by creating tech-free focus periods for reading, learning, and meaningful conversations. These practices may seem simple, but they are helping us become more intentional about how we engage with technology rather than allowing technology to dictate our attention.

Navigating AI in 6–12 Education

Another presentation that resonated deeply with me explored the role of artificial intelligence in K–12 education. Rather than focusing solely on concerns about academic dishonesty, the presenters reframed AI as an essential literacy skill that students will need for their future careers and lives.

What stood out most was the emphasis on evaluating learning processes rather than simply assessing final products. By designing learning experiences that require students to document, critique, and reflect on their use of AI, educators can encourage authentic learning while reducing opportunities for misuse.

As both an educator and a parent, I found this perspective refreshing. It shifted my thinking away from fear and restriction toward preparation and empowerment. The presentation reinforced the idea that the goal is not to shield students from emerging technologies but to equip them with the skills and judgment needed to use them responsibly and effectively.

Accessibility and the Social Model of Disability

The presentation on the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) was equally impactful. What resonated most strongly was the discussion of the shift from the Medical Model to the Social Model of disability.

For many years, disability was often viewed as an individual problem requiring individuals to adapt to existing systems. The Social Model challenges this perspective by arguing that barriers arise when environments, systems, and technologies are designed without considering human diversity. This framework places responsibility on society to create accessible and inclusive spaces.

This idea has significant implications for educational technology. Learners using a screen reader is not limited by their disability; they are limited when digital resources are poorly designed and inaccessible. Accessibility, therefore, should not be treated as an afterthought or compliance requirement. It should be embedded into the design process from the very beginning.

This presentation reinforced my belief that effective educational design is inclusive by default. Rather than asking individuals to adapt to our systems, we should be creating systems that welcome and support all learners from the start.

Final Thoughts

Together, these presentations highlighted a common theme: technology itself is neither the problem nor the solution. Its impact depends on how thoughtfully we design, implement, and engage with it. Whether addressing attention in a digital age, preparing students for an AI-driven future, or building accessible learning environments, educators have a responsibility to create conditions that promote meaningful, equitable, and empowering learning experiences.

Reflection: Technology Adoption as a Human Evolution

This week's exploration of technology adoption models and learning theories offered a vital continuation of our ongoing conversations around digital innovation. I found myself reflecting on the human and pedagogical considerations that make technology meaningful in education.

The Cognitive Buffet: Constructivism, Scaffolding, and Choice

When analyzing how we adopt new technologies, Constructivism reminds me that the value of technology lies not in delivering content, but in creating opportunities for students to explore and build understanding. As we saw in the study of "Teacher Tony," technology thrives when it serves as a vehicle for student-led discovery and real-world problem-solving.

While the Universal Design for Learning "dinner party" analogy — preparing a rich buffet instead of acting like a frazzled short-order cook — is a concept from outside our specific readings, it aligns perfectly with the idea of scaffolding. By providing flexible digital tools, we offer the "structurally supportive interactions" needed to construct a learning buffet. This allows learners to self-differentiate and stay within their Zone of Proximal Development, that "sweet spot" where a task is challenging but entirely achievable with the right support.

The Heart of Adoption: Protecting Flow and Community

However, there is a critical boundary to this buffet: Cognitive Load Theory. When a digital interface is cluttered, a student's limited working memory is instantly pushed to capacity by distracting stimuli. If we don't minimize these extraneous distractions, we prevent students from entering a state of Flow — that hyper-focused "optimal experience" where they are fully engaged in the creative process.

To achieve this focus, the digital environment must also feel safe. Frameworks like the Community of Inquiry and the Fully Online Learning Community model highlight that social presence is a prerequisite for cognitive presence. If we ignore the human connection and focus purely on pushing content, deep learning stalls. For technology to be successfully adopted, the digital space must feel like a community of "critical friends" where students, peers, and instructors can show up as real people, sharing ideas and emotions without fear.

The Invisible Power of Tools

Finally, Activity Theory provided a healthy dose of reality by reminding us that tools are active "actors" in our ecosystem. They are never neutral; they actively reshape human behavior. Just as the invention of air conditioning quietly pulled neighborhoods off their front porches and isolated them indoors, the digital tools we choose for our classrooms will fundamentally rewrite our cultural rules, relationships, and divisions of labor.

Conclusion

Technology adoption is never just a technical milestone; it is a human evolution. To truly innovate, we must stop focusing on mastering the software and start focusing on protecting the cognitive harmony and social safety of the people using it.

Week 6Designing for Digital Pedagogy
June 15 – June 21, 2026
Week 6 June 21, 2026 reflection

Teaching Effectively in a Digital Age

What does it look like to teach effectively in a digital age? Reflecting on our recent class materials, it's clear that the answer goes far beyond just handing students a device; it's about entirely rewriting the learning experience itself. My exploration of modern digital pedagogy has centered on four interconnected pillars: standardized frameworks, foundational digital accessibility, innovative pedagogical practices, and the intentional nuance of gaming.

Grounding Practice in Transformation

To rewrite this experience, we must first change how we view the tools themselves. True technology integration requires moving past basic enhancements and aiming for genuine educational transformation. Through the lens of the SAMR model (Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, Redefinition), it's clear that meaningful digital learning is about more than just swapping paper for a screen. Instead, our goal should be to modify or redefine tasks entirely, leveraging technology to design learning experiences that were previously impossible. The ISTE standards support this shift, providing a foundational framework that inspires student agencies and drives these innovative teaching practices.

The Inclusive Foundation

However, designing transformative learning experiences is meaningless if those experiences are not reachable by every student. This is why standardized frameworks must be built upon a strict standard of digital accessibility. It is a baseline requirement, not a design afterthought. Mindful choices such as crafting clear alt text, organizing content with proper heading structures, and ensuring strong color contrast are absolutely essential. These practices transcend basic aesthetics; they guarantee machine readability and actively dismantle systemic barriers to learning. By embedding accessibility into our initial design framework, we cultivate an equitable, high-quality educational experience that fully supports individuals with visual impairments or diverse processing needs.

Open-Access and Critical AI

Once an equitable foundation is established, we can confidently introduce innovative pedagogical practices that challenge how students interact with the wider world. For instance, open-access pedagogy completely redefines student engagement. Moving away from disposable assignments toward collaborative, living textbooks provide students with an authentic, global audience that inspires genuine pride and investment in their learning.

We see this exact same strategic shift in how we handle emerging technologies like AI. By treating ChatGPT as an object of critique rather than a shortcut or a threat, we can have students dissect AI-generated content for biases and errors. This approach effectively turns a simple text generator into a powerful vehicle for building subject-matter expertise and information fluency.

The Power of Play

This same spirit of active, critical participation carries over into how we leverage modern student culture through play. Just as we use AI as an active tool rather than a passive shortcut, understanding the distinction between gamification and Games-Based Learning allows us to design deliberate, immersive environments. While gamification layers game-like elements, think leaderboards, points, progress bars, and badges across an existing curriculum to incentivize progress, GBL embeds the lesson directly inside a single game.

The Quest to Learn school beautifully bridges these two ideas by immersing students in complex, challenge-based missions where learning is scaffolded through interconnected quests. This approach honors the reality that game design is the modern generation's mode of discourse. By meeting students where they are, we can leverage these gaming frameworks to help them think deeper, grasp abstract concepts, and become active problem solvers.

The Final Takeaway

This journey has reminded me that technology in the classroom is so much more than just slides and screens, it's about giving students a voice. Whether that looks like a digital breakout, a WebQuest, or a collaborative game design project, our ultimate mission doesn't change. We design the space, step back, and hand over the keys. Ultimately, we want to empower them to steer their own learning journeys, long after they leave our classrooms.
